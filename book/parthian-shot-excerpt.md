Bouldering is just what we call rock climbing on smaller - well - boulders. You don't use ropes, you usually just put a mat or two down and climb. We call the routes 'problems', boulder problems. Every established route gets named by the first ascensionist, and they give it a grade. Other climbers who do the route can downgrade it if they think it's easier, it's a neat enough system. Most of the rock in the Peak District is gritstone. It's porous. Makes conditions a huge factor in how you experience the climb. If it's hot, a climb can feel impossible; the feet won't grip, the sweat from your hands will rip through the chalk we use to maximise friction immediately. You really don't need warm fingers for the problem. It's all in the feet. But I always warm up regardless. I have a little wooden block. It's shaped like a small climbing hold and has two edges. Eight mil and four mil. It has a short rope lashed around it. I wrap the rope around my foot, grab the eight mil edge and just pull gently. It helps get blood to your fingers, activates all your tendons and you can get used to just pulling. When you've popped tendons and pulleys, you like to warm up.

There are so many types of climbing. I'm sorry but I've no doubt I'll end up telling you more than you need to know. But it is important. Within bouldering you could split it mainly between slab and overhanging. Overhanging is obvious, the rock comes out over you, you need to be very strong, very powerful. Slab is the opposite. The rock leans away from you. Many slab problems are made harder by simply not being allowed to use your hands. Just carefully placing your feet and balancing your way up the wall. I love slab. Crimpy means the holds are just sharp, small edges. There are bunch of ways to grip a hold. Most commonly for me on the woody, I use a 'full-crimp' grip.
